# Fourier Neural Operator (FNO)

> **Li et al. (2021)**
>
> **Fourier Neural Operator for Parametric Partial Differential Equations**
>
> **International Conference on Learning Representations (ICLR 2021)**

---

## Why I Read This Paper

Fourier Neural Operator (FNO) is one of the most influential architectures in Scientific Machine Learning for solving PDEs efficiently. Unlike DeepONet, which learns operators using separate Branch and Trunk networks, FNO performs operator learning directly in the Fourier domain through spectral convolution.

I read this paper to understand how neural operators can accelerate PDE-based simulations and how FNO can be applied to semiconductor TCAD surrogate models for fast device simulation.

---

## Problem

Many scientific and engineering problems require repeatedly solving Partial Differential Equations (PDEs).

Examples include:

- Fluid dynamics
- Heat transfer
- Structural mechanics
- Electromagnetic simulation
- Semiconductor device simulation (TCAD)

Traditional numerical methods such as the Finite Element Method (FEM) or Finite Difference Method (FDM) provide accurate solutions but become computationally expensive when solving many PDE instances with different boundary conditions or material parameters.

Although DeepONet learns nonlinear operators, its computational complexity increases with spatial resolution because predictions are generated point-by-point.

The challenge is to develop an operator learning framework that is both computationally efficient and resolution-independent.

---

## Key Idea

Fourier Neural Operator performs operator learning in the **frequency domain**.

Instead of learning local spatial relationships directly, FNO transforms feature maps into Fourier space using the Fast Fourier Transform (FFT), performs global convolution on low-frequency modes, and transforms them back into physical space.

```
Input Function
      │
      ▼
 Fourier Transform (FFT)
      │
      ▼
Spectral Convolution
      │
      ▼
Inverse FFT
      │
      ▼
Output Function
```

By learning global interactions in the frequency domain, FNO efficiently captures long-range dependencies while remaining independent of mesh resolution.

---

## Method

Each Fourier Layer consists of four main steps.

### 1. Lift Layer

The input function is projected into a higher-dimensional feature space.

---

### 2. Fourier Transform

Apply Fast Fourier Transform (FFT):

\[
\mathcal{F}(x)
\]

to convert spatial features into frequency components.

---

### 3. Spectral Convolution

Instead of applying convolution in physical space, convolution is performed only on selected low-frequency Fourier modes.

High-frequency components are truncated to reduce computational cost while preserving important global information.

---

### 4. Inverse Fourier Transform

Transform the processed frequency representation back into physical space using

\[
\mathcal{F}^{-1}
\]

Multiple Fourier Layers are stacked to approximate the nonlinear operator.

---

## Equation

The Fourier Layer is expressed as

\[
v_{l+1}(x)
=
\sigma
\left(
Wv_l(x)
+
\mathcal{F}^{-1}
\left(
R
\cdot
\mathcal{F}(v_l)
\right)
\right)
\]

where

- \(v_l\): Feature map at layer \(l\)
- \(W\): Linear transformation
- \(\mathcal{F}\): Fourier Transform
- \(R\): Learnable spectral weights
- \(\sigma\): Activation function

Unlike CNNs, the convolution is performed globally in Fourier space rather than locally in image space.

---

## Advantages

- Learns nonlinear operators directly.
- Resolution-independent after training.
- Captures global spatial interactions naturally.
- Faster inference than conventional numerical solvers.
- Excellent scalability to high-resolution simulations.
- Achieves state-of-the-art performance on many PDE benchmarks.
- Highly suitable for surrogate modeling of complex physical systems.

---

## Limitations

- Requires a large dataset generated by numerical simulations.
- Works best for structured grid data.
- Less effective for irregular geometries without modifications.
- Physical constraints are not explicitly enforced as in PINNs.
- Interpretability is lower than traditional numerical methods.

---

## Key Takeaways

- Fourier Neural Operator significantly improves the efficiency of operator learning.
- Spectral convolution enables global receptive fields with relatively low computational cost.
- Unlike DeepONet, FNO naturally supports high-resolution prediction after training.
- FNO is considered one of the leading architectures for AI-based PDE solvers.
- It has strong potential for accelerating semiconductor TCAD simulations and enabling Virtual R&D.
